1. Executive Summary

The Nesbitt Centre (TNC) requires a digital learning management system to support its 5-year Entrepreneurship Programme (EP) for adults with mild intellectual disabilities and autism. The system must track learner progression across 20 sub-skills, support observation-based assessment, generate compliance reports for funders, and be genuinely easy for non-technical teaching staff to use on their phones during live sessions.

This proposal outlines an implementation approach built on Zite (a no-code app platform used by Harvard and UC Berkeley) and Google Sheets (as the data layer), delivering a fully configured EP Operating System at a total implementation cost of HKD $80,000. Ongoing platform costs are just USD $854 per year (approximately HKD $6,700/year) with zero per-student licensing fees.

Xchool operates at the intersection of education and AI. We configure platforms rather than build custom software, which means lower cost, faster delivery, and no vendor lock-in. The platform architecture is designed to replicate across TNC's other programmes: EP is the pilot that proves the approach, and success here builds the foundation for TNC's entire digital learning infrastructure at a fraction of the initial cost.

A working prototype already exists and is available for demonstration.

2. Understanding of Requirements

The EP Programme

The Entrepreneurship Programme is a 5-year cyclical curriculum structured around five core skill areas:

  1. Concepts: What is entrepreneurship?
  2. Ideation: Self-awareness, strengths, problems, services
  3. Planning: Structuring ideas into actionable plans
  4. Finance and Pricing: Understanding costs, pricing, and financial basics
  5. Target Customers and Marketing: Identifying audiences and reaching them

These five skills break down into 20 sub-skills, each tracked through developmental stages:

NEW → Consolidate → Apply → Transfer

Learners follow a "Led to Leader" trajectory across five programme rounds. Success is measured by understanding, engagement, and participation, not independence alone. This distinction is central to the platform's design: the system tracks how learners interact with and absorb material, not whether they can perform tasks without support.

What TNC Needs

  • A single platform for session planning, observation logging, and learner tracking
  • Mobile-first design that teaching staff can use during sessions, not after, when details have faded
  • Skill progression tracking mapped to the EP curriculum framework
  • Compliance-ready reporting for board and funder review
  • Photo and video evidence capture linked to observations
  • A system that runs on infrastructure TNC already uses (Google Workspace)
  • Minimal ongoing costs and no per-learner fees
  • A tool the team will actually want to use, not one they have to be reminded about

The Adoption Challenge

TNC's teaching team is young, capable, and comfortable with technology. But structured digital progress tracking is new territory: the team has never had a purpose-built system for it before. The biggest risk facing this project is not whether the technology works. It is whether the team adopts it consistently enough to unlock its value.

This proposal is designed around that reality. Every architectural decision, every interface choice, every element of the implementation plan is shaped by one question: will teachers actually use this, every day, without being asked?

The answer has to be yes, not through policy enforcement, but because the tool genuinely makes their work easier. If logging a skill observation takes longer than it would on paper, the system fails. If generating a report requires a manual, the system fails. If opening the app feels like a chore, the system fails.

The platform described in this proposal is built so that none of those things happen.

3. Why Not an Off-the-Shelf Platform?

TNC's requirements sit at an unusual intersection: SEN-specific skill tracking, observation-based assessment for adults, structured curriculum progression, compliance reporting, and a team that needs something genuinely simple. Several established platforms come close, but none quite fit.

Seesaw is a well-built tool, but it is designed for K-12 children. Its interface, language, and interaction patterns are built for primary school students. For adult learners with intellectual disabilities, this creates a tone problem: the platform would feel infantilising rather than empowering, which runs counter to the dignity and agency at the heart of TNC's programmes.

Canvas is a robust LMS widely used in higher education. However, it is built for academic institutions (lecture-based courses, assignment submission, grading rubrics). It does not support observation-based assessment, SEN progression frameworks, or the kind of flexible skill tracking EP requires. Its complexity demands technical administration, and recent security incidents have raised questions about data governance for smaller organisations.

Google Classroom is free and familiar, but it is fundamentally a classroom management tool, not a programme management system. It has no custom skill tracking, no SEN progression frameworks, no embedded AI tools, and no way to map the kind of multi-year developmental journey EP learners follow.

Moodle shares many of Canvas's strengths and limitations. It is powerful, open-source, and built for universities. It also requires dedicated technical administration, significant configuration effort, and ongoing server management: resources TNC does not have and should not need to acquire.

Toddle is a strong, modern platform with genuinely good design. It is also priced at USD $7,000+ per year, with per-student licensing that makes scaling expensive. For TNC's budget and programme size, the cost structure does not work, and the platform is still oriented toward mainstream education rather than SEN-specific adult learning.

These are good tools for their intended audiences. But TNC's needs are specific: adult SEN learners, observation-based tracking, a non-technical team, compliance reporting for Hong Kong funders, and a budget that cannot absorb five-figure annual licensing fees. The EP Operating System is purpose-built for exactly this context.

4. Proposed Solution

Approach

Rather than purchasing a traditional LMS platform (typically HKD $55,000+/year in licensing alone) or commissioning a custom software build (high cost, long timeline, ongoing developer dependency), this proposal takes a third path: a fully configured platform built on proven, low-cost tools TNC already has access to.

The EP Operating System is built on Zite, a no-code application platform used by institutions including Harvard and UC Berkeley. Data is stored in Google Sheets within TNC's own Google Workspace, meaning TNC retains full ownership and access to all programme data at all times, with no vendor lock-in on the data layer.

This is a configured platform, not a custom software build. The distinction matters: custom builds require ongoing developer involvement for every change. A configured platform can be maintained and adjusted by trained staff using Zite's visual editor. No code, no developer, no waiting. If a curriculum module changes or a new skill area is added, TNC's own team can update the app directly.

Platform Architecture

  • Zite: Application layer (interface, navigation, workflows)
  • Google Sheets: Data layer (learner records, session logs, curriculum data)
  • Google Drive: File storage (photos, videos, documents)
  • AI Tools: Embedded quiz generation, progress report drafting, comprehension checks

Authentication is handled via Google account login. Teachers sign in with the Google Workspace account they already use every day: one tap, no new passwords, no new accounts. If you can unlock your phone, you can get into the EP system.

Designed to Be Easy

Ease of use is not a feature of this platform. It is the design principle everything else is built on.

The interface is intentionally warm and welcoming, not clinical, not academic, not cluttered. Big buttons. Clear labels. Generous spacing. Every feature is reachable in two or three taps from the home screen. The visual design borrows from the apps people already use and enjoy, not from enterprise software they tolerate.

There is no manual. The in-app onboarding walkthrough teaches the system as you use it: each screen explains itself the first time you see it, then gets out of the way. New staff members can be productive within their first session.

The honest benchmark: if you can use WhatsApp, you can use this. The interaction patterns are deliberately familiar (tap to open, tap to log, tap to save). The learning curve is measured in minutes, not days.

This matters because the platform only delivers value if people use it. A powerful system that sits unused is worse than no system at all. Every design decision in the EP Operating System is filtered through one question: will a busy teacher, mid-session, with a phone in one hand, actually do this? If the answer is not an obvious yes, the feature is redesigned until it is.

5. What This Is Not

To set clear expectations:

  • This is not a custom software build. There is no bespoke codebase to maintain. The platform is configured using established tools, which means lower risk, lower cost, and no dependency on a single developer.
  • This is not a static website or document repository. It is an interactive operating system for running the EP programme day-to-day.
  • This is not a replacement for teaching. The platform supports educators by reducing admin burden and surfacing learner insights. It does not automate instruction.
  • This is not locked to one consultant. The data lives in TNC's Google Workspace. The app runs on a commercial platform. If TNC ever wants to change partner or manage the system internally, everything is transferable.
  • This is not a one-programme investment. The architecture is designed to replicate. EP is the proving ground; success here builds the foundation for TNC's other programmes.

9. Driving Adoption: The Real Success Factor

A platform is only as good as its adoption. TNC could have the most sophisticated learning management system in the world: if teachers do not use it consistently, it delivers nothing. This section outlines how the EP Operating System is specifically designed to make adoption inevitable rather than aspirational.

Why Adoption Is the Central Challenge

TNC's team has never used structured digital progress tracking. That is not a criticism; it simply means this represents a genuine shift in how work gets done. The risk is not that the technology fails. The risk is that a busy teacher, mid-session, decides it is easier to jot something on paper and "log it later", and later never comes.

Everything about the platform is designed to prevent that moment from happening.

How the Platform Drives Its Own Adoption

Phone-first by design. Teachers log observations during sessions, not after. The phone is already in their hand. The app opens in one tap via Google login. Logging a skill observation takes less time than writing a note on paper, and the data is immediately structured, searchable, and reportable.

One-tap actions for common tasks. The most frequent actions (logging an observation, tagging a skill, attaching a photo) are single-tap operations from the session screen. No menus to navigate, no forms to fill out, no dropdowns to scroll through.

Visual design that feels right. The interface does not look like enterprise software. It is warm, colourful, and friendly: designed to feel like something you choose to open, not something you are required to use. This is a deliberate design choice. Tools that feel pleasant get used. Tools that feel clinical get avoided.

In-app onboarding that teaches by doing. There is no training manual. No PowerPoint deck to sit through. The first time a teacher opens any screen, a brief walkthrough explains what it does and how to use it. After that, it stays out of the way. The goal is for new staff to be confident within their first session.

Immediate visible value. Teachers see the benefit within their first week: learner progress visualised, session history available at a glance, reports that used to take hours generated in minutes. The platform earns its place by making tangible work easier, not by promising future benefits.

Implementation Support for Adoption

Phase 2 of this proposal (Launch Support, Training and Handover) includes dedicated adoption support:

  • Hands-on training sessions designed around real scenarios, not abstract walkthroughs
  • First-week shadowing: available support during the first live sessions to build confidence
  • Quick-reference guides optimised for phone screens (though the goal is that nobody needs them)
  • Monthly check-ins during the retainer period specifically to review adoption patterns and address friction points early

What Successful Adoption Looks Like

The goal is simple and measurable: teachers using the platform daily because it genuinely makes their job easier, not because they have been told to.

When that happens, everything else follows: accurate data, meaningful reports, evidence of learner progress, compliance documentation that writes itself. Adoption is not a box to tick. It is the foundation that every other benefit rests on.

10. EP as Pilot: Building TNC's Digital Foundation

The Strategic View

This proposal is scoped to the Entrepreneurship Programme, but it is designed with TNC's broader needs in mind. EP is the pilot. The testing ground. The place to prove that this approach works, collect real evidence, and build internal confidence.

If the EP Operating System succeeds (if teachers adopt it, if the data flows, if reporting becomes easier, if learner progress becomes visible in ways it was not before), then the same platform architecture can expand to TNC's other programmes.

Why EP Is the Right Starting Point

EP has clear structure (5 skills, 20 sub-skills, defined progression stages), a motivated team, and an immediate need for better tracking. It is complex enough to be a genuine test of the platform's capabilities, but bounded enough to manage risk. Success here is credible evidence for the board that the approach works.

How Expansion Works

The technical architecture is built for replication:

  • Google Sheets data layer: each programme gets its own data structure, following the same patterns established in EP. No migration, no new infrastructure.
  • Zite app framework: the EP Operating System serves as a template. New programmes are configured by adapting the existing structure, not building from scratch. This is dramatically faster and cheaper than the initial build.
  • Shared components: learner profiles, reporting dashboards, and admin tools carry across programmes with minimal modification.

The Investment Case

The HKD $80,000 implementation cost builds three things:

  1. A working EP Operating System: immediate, tangible value for the programme launching in July
  2. A proven architecture: validated approach to digital learning management at TNC, with real usage data and evidence
  3. A replicable foundation: the template, patterns, and infrastructure for expanding to other programmes at a fraction of the initial cost

Expanding to a second programme after EP is proven would be significantly less expensive: the infrastructure exists, the patterns are established, and the team already knows how to use the tools. The first programme is the investment. Subsequent programmes are the return.

A Practical Roadmap

Year 1 (2026-2027): Launch EP, drive adoption, collect data, demonstrate value. Build the evidence base.

Year 2 (2027-2028): Review EP outcomes. If the approach is validated, begin planning expansion to one additional programme. Adapt the existing architecture rather than starting over.

Year 3 and beyond: Continue expanding programme by programme. Each addition strengthens the overall system and reduces the marginal cost of the next.

This is a strategic investment in TNC's digital learning infrastructure, not a one-off project cost for a single programme.
